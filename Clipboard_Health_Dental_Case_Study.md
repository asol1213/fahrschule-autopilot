## Scenario 1: Re-engaging Sarah — A Dental Hygienist Who Stopped Picking Up Shifts

### 1. My Introduction (First 30–60 Seconds)

"Hi Sarah, this is [Name] from Clipboard Health Dental. How are you doing today? ... Great — I appreciate you taking a minute to chat. I wanted to reach out because, honestly, you've been one of the standout hygienists in the Minneapolis area. The offices you worked with consistently gave you excellent ratings, and it was clear you were doing really great work out there. I noticed it's been about a month since your last shift, and I just wanted to check in — not to pressure you into anything, but because I genuinely want to make sure everything's okay on your end and see if there's anything I can help with. Is now a good time to talk for a few minutes?"

### 2. Questions I Would Ask

**Q1: "How have things been going for you lately — both personally and professionally?"**
*Why:* This is intentionally open-ended. I want to give Sarah space to share what's actually going on before I start guessing. The reason she stopped could be personal, professional, or something about the platform — and I won't know unless I listen first. People share more when they don't feel interrogated.

**Q2: "When you think back on your experience picking up shifts with us, what worked well for you — and what didn't?"**
*Why:* I want to understand her overall experience, not just the last interaction. This helps me identify whether the issue is a single bad event or a pattern of friction. It also signals that I care about her honest feedback, not just getting her back on the schedule.

**Q3: "Was there anything specific that happened around the time you stopped — maybe a shift that didn't go well, or something about the app or the process that frustrated you?"**
*Why:* If there was a specific trigger — a bad office experience, a payment issue, a scheduling conflict — I need to know about it. The first answer to "why did you stop?" is often surface-level. This question digs into the specifics without being accusatory.

**Q4: "Has anything changed in your schedule or work situation — like picking up a full-time position or adjusting your availability?"**
*Why:* Many hygienists use Clipboard for flexibility between permanent roles. If Sarah found a full-time job, that's a very different conversation than if she's still looking for shifts but chose a competitor or stopped for other reasons.

**Q5: "If you were to consider picking up shifts again, what would need to be different — or what would make it worth your time?"**
*Why:* This puts the ball in Sarah's court and lets her tell me what she actually needs. It also shifts the conversation from "why did you leave" to "what would bring you back," which is more forward-looking and productive.

**Q6: "Are you working with any other staffing platforms or agencies right now?"**
*Why:* If she's shifted to a competitor, I need to understand what they're offering that we're not. This isn't about being defensive — it's about understanding the competitive landscape and whether we have a genuine gap.

### 3. Possible Scenarios & How I'd Navigate Them

**Scenario A: Sarah got a full-time position.**
She found a permanent role at a dental office. This is positive news for her, and I'd respond accordingly: "That's great, Sarah — congratulations. I'm genuinely happy that worked out for you." I'd ask how she's liking it so far and let her know that if her situation ever changes — if the hours don't work out, if she wants to pick up extra weekend shifts for additional income, or if the role isn't what she expected — her profile is still active and she can jump back in anytime. No pressure, just an open door. I'd also ask if she knows any other hygienists who might be looking for flexible work, since a warm referral from a high-rated worker is valuable.

**Scenario B: She had a negative experience at an office.**
Maybe a shift went poorly — an unwelcoming staff, disorganized office, or a situation where she felt uncomfortable. I'd listen carefully without minimizing her experience: "I'm really sorry that happened, Sarah. That's not the experience we want you to have." I'd ask for the specific details so I can flag it internally and make sure other workers don't face the same issue. I'd let her know what concrete steps we'd take — whether that's blocking that office from her feed, following up with the office, or escalating internally. Then I'd offer to personally help her find shifts at offices with consistently high worker ratings so she feels confident going back.

**Scenario C: She's frustrated with the platform itself — pay, shift availability, or app issues.**
If Sarah feels the pay isn't competitive enough, or the shifts posted in her area dried up, or the app was glitchy, I'd validate that feedback: "That's really helpful to know — thank you for being honest about that." I'd share any relevant updates — new offices in her area, pay rate changes, or app improvements. If there's nothing concrete I can offer, I'd be transparent about that and commit to passing her feedback to the team rather than making promises I can't keep.

**Scenario D: Personal reasons — burnout, family situation, health.**
If something personal is going on, I'd keep it simple and human: "I totally understand, and I hope things are going okay for you." I wouldn't push. I'd let her know there's no expiration on her account and she's welcome back whenever she's ready. I'd ask if she'd like me to check in again in a few weeks or if she'd prefer to reach out on her own terms.

**Scenario E: She simply drifted away — no specific reason.**
Sometimes people just stop without a dramatic catalyst. Life gets busy. I'd acknowledge that and gently remind her of what she liked about the platform: "You mentioned before that the flexibility was a big deal for you — is that still something you value?" Sometimes a warm, genuine check-in is enough to reactivate someone who simply fell out of the habit.

### 4. Common Objections & How I'd Handle Them

**"I'm too busy right now."**
"That makes total sense — I'm not here to add anything to your plate. One thing I'll mention is that there's no minimum commitment. If there's even one day a month where you'd want to grab a shift, the option is there. But honestly, if now isn't the right time, that's completely fine. Would it be helpful if I checked back in with you in a month or two?"

**"The pay wasn't worth it."**
"I hear you, and I appreciate you telling me that directly. Can I ask — was it the hourly rate itself, or was it more about factoring in commute time or other costs? ... That's helpful context. [If rates have improved:] I should mention that we've seen rates in your area come up recently — I can show you what's currently posted if you're curious. [If not:] I want to be honest — I can't promise a rate change right now, but I'm going to share this feedback with our team because you're not the only one I've heard this from, and it matters."

**"I had a bad experience at one of the offices."**
"I'm sorry to hear that, and I want to take that seriously. Can you tell me a bit about what happened? ... Thank you for sharing that. Here's what I can do: I can make sure that office doesn't show up in your available shifts, and I'll flag this with our team so we can follow up. If you're open to it, I can also point you toward offices that consistently get positive feedback from hygienists — would that be helpful?"

**"I found something more stable / I have a permanent job now."**
"That's wonderful — stability is important, and I'm glad you found something that works. If you ever want to supplement your income with an extra shift here and there, we'll be here. And if you know any colleagues who are looking for flexibility, I'd love to connect with them — your recommendation would carry a lot of weight given how highly rated you were."

**"I just don't think temp work is for me."**
"That's a fair conclusion, and I respect that. Can I ask what about it didn't feel right? ... I appreciate your honesty. If anything changes down the road, your account will still be here. And thank you for giving us a real shot — the offices you worked with genuinely valued your work."

## Scenario 2: Re-engaging Bright Smiles Dental — An Office That Stopped Posting Shifts

### 1. My Introduction

"Hi, this is [Name] from Clipboard Health Dental. I'm hoping to speak with the office manager — is that you? ... Great, thanks for taking my call. I wanted to reach out because Bright Smiles has been one of our most consistent offices in the Minneapolis area, and the hygienists who've picked up shifts with you have had nothing but good things to say about your team. I noticed it's been about a month since your last posting, and I just wanted to check in — see how things are going and whether there's anything we can do on our end to better support you. Do you have a few minutes?"

### 2. Questions I Would Ask

**Q1: "How have things been going at the practice lately — busy season, any staffing changes?"**
*Why:* I want to understand the broader context of their business right now. If they hired a full-time hygienist, that's different from if they're slow on patients. This open-ended question lets them lead with what's top of mind.

**Q2: "When you were using Clipboard regularly, how did the experience work for your office — did the workers meet your expectations?"**
*Why:* I need to know if there was a quality issue. Even though workers left good feedback, the office may have had a different experience. If there was a mismatch, I need to surface that.

**Q3: "Was there a specific reason you stopped posting, or did the need just change?"**
*Why:* Direct but not aggressive. I'm trying to distinguish between "we don't need temp staff anymore" and "something went wrong." The answer shapes the entire rest of the conversation.

**Q4: "How are you handling coverage right now when someone calls in sick or you have a gap in the schedule?"**
*Why:* Even if they stopped posting with us, the underlying staffing challenge likely hasn't disappeared. This question reframes the conversation around their ongoing pain point rather than our platform.

**Q5: "Is there anything about the process — posting shifts, the workers you received, billing — that was frustrating or that you'd want us to improve?"**
*Why:* I want specific, actionable feedback. Offices often have small frustrations they never mention until someone asks directly. A billing confusion or an inconvenient posting process might be an easy fix that removes a real barrier.

**Q6: "What does your staffing picture look like over the next few months — any vacations, maternity leaves, or busy periods coming up?"**
*Why:* Even if they don't need temp staff today, they likely will. This plants the seed for future use and positions me as someone thinking ahead for them, not just reacting.

**Scenario A: They hired a permanent hygienist and no longer need temp coverage.**
"That's great — having a full team makes everything easier. Congrats on finding the right person. I'd love to stay in touch, though, because even offices with full staff sometimes need backup for vacations, sick days, or unexpected gaps. Would it be okay if I checked in with you periodically — not to sell you anything, but just so you know we're here when something comes up? It's a lot easier to post a shift with an account you already have set up than to scramble last-minute."

**Scenario B: They had a bad experience with a temp worker.**
"I'm really sorry to hear that — and thank you for telling me, because that's something I want to help fix. Can you walk me through what happened? ... That shouldn't have happened, and I understand why it would make you hesitant. Here's what I can do: I can flag that worker so they're not matched with your office again, and I can work with you to set preferences so you're getting hygienists with the specific experience and ratings you need. We have several highly-rated hygienists in your area who I think would be a much better fit — would you be open to trying one more shift with a worker I'd personally recommend?"

**Scenario C: They're using a different staffing agency or competitor.**
"That makes sense — you need to do what's best for your practice. Can I ask what's working well with the other service? ... That's helpful to know. [Address specific advantages if possible.] I'm not here to badmouth anyone, but I'd love the chance to earn your business back. Would you be open to posting one shift with us alongside your current solution — just to compare? No commitment beyond that."

**Scenario D: Budget constraints or cost concerns.**
"I totally understand — managing costs is a huge part of running a practice. Can I ask how the cost of using Clipboard compared to your other staffing options — or to the cost of being short-staffed on a busy day? ... Sometimes the cost of not having coverage — rescheduling patients, overworking your existing team, losing production — actually ends up being higher. I'm happy to walk you through the numbers for your specific situation if that would be useful."

**Scenario E: The person who managed the account left or the office changed management.**
"Ah, that makes sense — transitions can be hectic. I'd love to bring you up to speed on how the platform works and how your office was using it. The good news is your account is already set up and the previous feedback from workers was really positive, so you're starting from a strong position. Would you have 10 minutes for a quick walkthrough so you can see if it's something that would help you?"

**"We're fully staffed now — we don't need temp coverage."**

## Scenario 3: Activating Lakeside Family Dentistry — Signed Up But Never Posted

### 1. My Introduction

"Hi, this is [Name] from Clipboard Health Dental. I'm reaching out to the office manager at Lakeside Family Dentistry — is that you? ... Great, nice to connect with you. I noticed your office created an account with us a few weeks ago, and I just wanted to personally welcome you and see if there's anything I can help with. I know signing up and actually posting your first shift can feel like two very different steps, so I wanted to make sure you have everything you need. Do you have a couple of minutes?"

### 2. Questions I Would Ask

**Q1: "What originally prompted you to sign up — was there a specific staffing situation, or were you exploring options for the future?"**
*Why:* This is the most important question. It tells me whether they have an active, urgent need or were just kicking the tires. The answer determines my entire approach — urgency vs. education.

**Q2: "How are you currently handling it when a hygienist or assistant calls in sick or you have a scheduling gap?"**
*Why:* I want to understand their current pain and workaround. If they're scrambling to call personal contacts or cancelling patients, there's a real cost I can help them see. If they've never had coverage issues, the need may not be there yet.

**Q3: "Was there anything about the sign-up process or the platform that was confusing or that made you hesitate to post?"**
*Why:* There may be a simple friction point — maybe they didn't understand how to create a shift, or they weren't sure about pricing, or they got pulled away. If it's a usability issue, I can solve it on this call.

**Q4: "Do you have any concerns about bringing in a temp worker — maybe about quality, or how they'd fit in with your team for a day?"**
*Why:* A lot of offices are nervous about having a stranger work with their patients. This is often the unspoken barrier. By naming it directly, I give them permission to be honest about their hesitation.

**Q5: "What does your staffing situation look like right now — are you fully covered, or do you have any gaps coming up?"**
*Why:* If there's a near-term need, I can help them post right now while we're on the phone together. If not, I'm mapping their future needs so I can follow up at the right time.

**Q6: "Have you used temp staffing before — either through an agency or through an app like ours?"**
*Why:* If they're completely new to temp staffing, they'll need more hand-holding and reassurance. If they've used agencies before, I can position Clipboard against what they already know — faster, more flexible, same-day pay for workers (which attracts higher-quality candidates).

That way, if something comes up again, you'll be ready to go."

## Final Thoughts

Across all three scenarios, my approach comes down to the same core principles:

1. **Listen before you pitch.** Every conversation starts with understanding, not selling. The person on the other end has a reason for their behavior, and my job is to understand it — genuinely — before I try to change it.

2. **Be honest about what I can and can't do.** If I don't have a solution to their problem, I say so. Overselling creates short-term numbers and long-term damage. Honesty builds the kind of trust that brings people back.

3. **Make it easy to say yes.** Lower the stakes. One shift. Five minutes of their time. A trial with no commitment. The biggest barrier to re-engagement is usually inertia, not opposition — so I make the next step as small and risk-free as possible.

4. **Own the outcome.** A call that doesn't move someone forward isn't a successful call, no matter how polite the conversation was. I'm thinking about what success looks like before I pick up the phone, and I'm adjusting in real time based on what I hear.

5. **Respect the "no."** Not every conversation will end in a win, and that's okay. A respectful "no" today can become a "yes" in three months — but only if I didn't burn the relationship by pushing too hard the first time.
